From Data Towards Information

31 08 2021

#Preliminaries:
knitr::opts_chunk$set( message=FALSE, warning=FALSE) #echo = FALSE,

rm(list=ls())
library(readxl)
library(tidyverse)

Situation

Most people know Winston Churchill’s famous quote “I only believe in statistics that I doctored myself.” Nowadays, as the amount and availability of information is increasing tremendously every day, correlates of this statement become more and more apparent. The ability to properly derive conclusions from data is both key and obstacle for decision makers such as politicians, businesses and ordinary people. This trend is reinforced as social media competes heavily for our attention with pictures, facts and stories, and our resources for verification are usually very limited. Good news is that it can certainly be possible to come up with more evidence based decisions, especially as data availability, software and hardware issues become less problem than ever before. But how to separate wheat from chaff? One step on the path, in addition to stay calm will certainly be proper data literacy and transparency.

Help

Good reads on how (not) to do statistical analysis are for example provided by Reinhart (2015) or Spiegelhalter (2019), whereas Kahneman (2011) discusses cognitive challenges in decision making, for example people’s immanent inability to digest probabilistic information. Analytical tools such as statistics software R allow powerful data analysis and, in conjunction with the R-markdown framework, to present results together with the used code. Platforms like R-Bloggers facilitate developments in reproducible data analysis. Such possibilities, in parallel with adequate habits of communication (eg. Watzlawick (2018)) enable cultivating sound (public) discussion of data driven decision making. Being able to do the work provides you with a range of skills from exploring and summarizing data structures, over predicting likely outcomes for new instances (eg. Kuhn and Johnson (2013)), to assessing causal relationships (eg. Pearl (2019)).

Example

As a start, let’s see how to transcend data into information with just a few lines of code: Data is provided by the worldhappiness report 2021. The publisher collects and summarizes data on individual “self stated” happiness across a lot of countries. We read the data

#data from https://worldhappiness.report/ed/2021/
data_in <- read_excel("DataPanelWHR2021C2.xls")

#show data: 
head(data_in)

and manipulate it using the popular R-tidyverse framework in order to allow a regional as well as the developmental perspective on perceived happiness across the world. The resulting graph is inspired by Healy (2018). We select data from 2010 to 2020 and add the constraint that a country must be observable at least during 5 years. The resulting graph, a map of the world’s happiness across space and time, is sorted with regard a country’s average happiness:

data_in %>%
  filter(year %in% c(2010:2020)) %>% #look at data from 2010 to 2020
  group_by(`Country name`) %>%
  summarize(happiness_mean=mean(`Life Ladder`), n=n()) %>% #mean happiness by country
  filter(n>=5) %>% #minimum 5 observations per country
  mutate(rank=rank(-happiness_mean))  %>% #create country specific happiness rank
  inner_join(data_in, by=("Country name"))  %>% #combine with yearly data
  mutate(year=as.factor(year), `Country name`=fct_reorder(factor(`Country name`), -rank)) %>% #order countries
  filter(year %in% c(2010:2020)) %>%
  ggplot(aes(y=`Country name`, x=year,  fill=`Life Ladder`)) + #create graph
  scale_fill_viridis(option="inferno") +
  geom_tile(colour="white") +
theme_minimal(base_size = 8) +
  theme(axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 45, hjust=1)) +
  labs(title="Hierarchy and development of happiness", fill="Happiness score",
       y="Country", x="Year")

Not very unexpectedly, the nordic countries are at the top of the list, whereas African and Oriental countries are at the bottom. We see that average happiness is a relatively stable phenomenon, overall. Among others, the most happy country, Denmark, also had an decrease in average happiness in 2020. Was this due to the Covid-19 pandemic? Interestingly, in the same year average happiness increased in Germany compared to 2019. Did Germans had a good time working from home, or does this finding suggest that ones perceived happiness is evaluated also relative to other countries? (In Germany the impact of Covid-19 was less severe than in other countries from an epidemiological point of view.) For our map of happiness another lesson can probably be learned from real maps: White spaces, ie. unknown territory or missing observations in the language of data science, are often very interesting, and worth exploring. Generally, a lot of whites spaces can be seen in 2020, the Covid-19 year, in which there were lots of barriers for data collection. Also, white spaces in Syria from 2016 onwards probably can tell us a lot. Looking at Afghanistan, second last country at the bottom of the list before Rwanda, we see that after a relatively good year in 2010, happiness decreased steadily afterwards. Looking at extreme values seems to be a potentially fruitful enterprise, when one is interested in increasing welfare of nations. An important question is about the most effective options how countries can achieve better outcomes. Ideally, one would look for internal or external factors that play a crucial role in people’s happiness and build policies around it. Hopefully, analytical skills are used properly in this context, enhancing economic prosperity and freedom of mankind, allowing people to flourish, and to live a healthy and happy life.
